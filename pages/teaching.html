Teaching

Quantitative Biology

For the past three years, I've TA'd the biology department's one-week Quantitative Biology Bootcamp (AS.020.607). In 2020, I also TA'd the fall semester Quantitative Biology Lab course (AS.020.617).

These two courses introduce first-year PhD students — most of whom have no computational experience — to coding and computational biology. They learn Python and bash, and apply their skills to essential biological analyses like sequence alignment, GWAS, and RNA-seq.

Quant Bio was the class that taught me the foundational skills I needed to join a computational lab, so it has a special place in my heart.

Quant Bio during the pandemic

In the first year of the pandemic, my fellow TAs and I also converted Quant Bio Bootcamp & Lab into online/hybrid format for remote learning. This was especially challenging because these hands-on, coding-based courses normally rely on in-person interaction to debug and explain concepts.

Here are some of the online/hybrid Quant Bio innovations that I'm most proud of:

  • In normal live-coding sessions, we have students put a pink sticky note on their laptop to indicate that they need help, or a green sticky note if they've finished coding.
    • To replicate this experience, we added pink and green sticky note reacts to the Quant Bio Slack workspace, and asked students to react depending on their progress.
  • Helping individual students debug during live-coding sessions was stressful and time sensitive. This usually involved seeing a help message in the main Slack channel, and then DM'ing them to ask about their error.
    • To avoid multiple TAs messaging the same student, we made Slack reacts of each of our faces, which we used to indicate that one of us had seen a message and was handling it.

Human Genome Variation Lab

In 2020, I was awarded a Technology Fellowship from the Johns Hopkins Center for Educational Resources.

I'm partnering with Kate Weaver, another PhD student in the McCoy lab, to develop open-source modules for introducing undergraduates to computational genetics. These modules will be used to teach Human Genome Variation Lab (AS.020.319).

The modules, available as both an online textbook and "starter code" R Markdown files, adapt data from recent papers to:

  • Introduce key human genetics concepts
  • Teach the basics of coding in R
  • Allow for hands-on learning through built-in coding exercises and homework
